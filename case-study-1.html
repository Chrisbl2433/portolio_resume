Worth Paying For — Shaping a Subscription from Zero

A food-delivery marketplace wanted to launch a paid monthly subscription alongside its core on-demand ordering — but the value proposition was a blank page. Nobody yet knew what would make people pay, at what price, or in which markets. I led the 0→1, multi-market research that defined the proposition, prioritised the benefits, and set the pricing band before anything was built.

0→1 value-proposition research across 12 markets — qualitative discovery plus KANO prioritisation (n=300/market) — that defined what the subscription should include, what people would pay, and where to launch first.

Context

The business wanted a new recurring-revenue subscription to sit alongside on-demand ordering, but the proposition was undefined: which benefits would actually drive sign-up and retention, what monthly price would feel fair, and whether appetite held across very different markets. Loading the wrong bundle — or mispricing it — risked an expensive launch that churned. I was brought in to answer, with evidence, what the subscription should be.

My role

  • Lead researcher — designed and ran the multi-market programme end-to-end.
  • 0→1 partner — worked hand-in-glove with PM, PMM, Marketing, Strategy, and the Engineering director to turn insight into a prioritised feature set and price.
  • Synthesiser — translated qual and quant into a single value proposition the whole cross-functional team aligned on.

Approach

A three-phase programme moved from understanding the space, to prioritising and pricing the proposition, to validating the experience before build.

Phase 1 · Generative discovery

In-depth interviews plus in-context (ethnographic) observation — participants recruited and screened across 4 key markets (n=8 per market) — to understand how people think about delivery, where cost and friction bite, and what would make a subscription genuinely worth it.

Phase 2 · Prioritise & price

Concept testing (n=8 per key market) of candidate benefit bundles, plus a KANO survey with samples sized for market-level reads (n=300/market across 12 markets) to classify features and find the acceptable price band.

Phase 3 · Validate before build

Iterative prototype and usability testing of the sign-up and subscription-management flows before production.

Why KANO

KANO separates what customers merely like from what actually drives willingness to subscribe — sorting features into basic, performance, and delight (plus "indifferent"). That stopped the team loading the bundle with low-value perks, and paired with the qualitative work it explained the why behind each classification.

Findings

  • ✅Speed beat savings. What people valued most was fast, reliable delivery — not coupons or discounts.
  • 💸Fee removal was the true must-have. No delivery fee and no service fee registered as basic expectations for a paid tier — their absence would collapse the value perception.
  • 🍽️Restaurant selection was a hidden driver. The breadth and quality of available restaurants strongly shaped whether the subscription felt worth paying for — beyond the subscription mechanics themselves.
  • 🎁Simple loyalty resonated; gimmicks didn't. A legible mechanic (e.g. every 4th order free) landed as a delight; vouchers and complex discount schemes were low-priority or indifferent.
  • 🌍Appetite and price varied by market. Willingness to pay and the must-have bundle differed enough to require a market-tiered go-to-market, not one global rollout.

Outcome & Impact

The research defined the value proposition and prioritised the bundle — fee removal, delivery-speed reliability, and a simple loyalty mechanic, over low-value perks. It set an evidence-based price band per market, shaped a market-tiered launch sequence (leading where appetite and willingness-to-pay were strongest), and de-risked the build by validating the flows before production. The recommended bundle and market-tiered pricing were adopted for launch, and in the lead markets subscriber uptake ran ahead of the business case.
