Launch-Ready — Running the Quality Bar for a New Platform

For six months I designed and ran — single-handedly — the usability programme that set the product-quality bar for a new spatial-computing productivity platform. Two research rounds a month, in-lab and remote, moderated and unmoderated, spanning the full range of product research: improving the live experience, informing new features, validating what was ready to ship — and blocking what wasn't.

Context

A new spatial-computing productivity platform was moving toward launch with a high quality bar and many features maturing in parallel — but no consistent, repeatable way to judge whether each was actually good enough to ship. I stood up and ran the usability programme that became that bar: the single mechanism the team used to decide what shipped, what improved, and what waited.

My role

  • Programme lead & sole researcher — designed the programme, wrote the plans, moderated, analysed, and reported; ran the entire cadence solo.
  • Quality-bar owner — defined the standard each new feature had to clear before launch.
  • Cross-functional partner — turned findings into shipped changes, go/no-go calls, and re-scopes with PM, Design, and Engineering.

Approach

The programme ran as a continuous operating model rather than a set of one-off studies.

Cadence & setup

2 rounds/month for 6 months; a mix of in-lab moderated sessions (6–8 users each) and remote unmoderated tests (20–30 users each), matched to the question and the speed needed.

The quality bar

Every new feature scored against a consistent usability standard before it could ship.

Findings → action

Each round fed prioritised fixes, go/no-go calls, and re-scopes straight to PM, Design, and Engineering.

Why a continuous, mixed-mode programme

Because the product was brand new and shipping features and improvements on an ongoing basis, research had to align with the roadmap rather than run as one-off studies. A twice-monthly cadence — deep moderated lab sessions paired with fast remote unmoderated tests — kept usability evidence flowing in step with what engineering was building, so each new feature and improvement had input at the point it was needed.

What the programme delivered

  • ✅Set the quality bar. Gave the team a single, consistent standard for "good enough to ship," replacing subjective debate.
  • 🛠️Improved the live experience. Drove continuous UX/UI fixes to core navigation, controls, and input targeting, round after round.
  • ✨Informed new features. Shaped the design of new features before build — including input and multi-window/layout interactions.
  • 🚦Validated what was ready. Gave confident go decisions on features that cleared the bar — 4 new features shipped.
  • 🛑Blocked what wasn't. Recommended holding 2 features that failed the bar, and caught 3 launch blockers before release.

Outcome & Impact

The programme became the team's quality bar for launch — the mechanism the org trusted to decide what shipped. 4 new features launched after clearing it; UX/UI improvements shipped every round. 2 features were held back as not-ready, and 3 launch blockers were caught before release. And it ran entirely solo across six months at a twice-monthly cadence — research infrastructure, not just a series of studies. Every feature that cleared the bar shipped on schedule, with no major post-launch usability regressions flagged on them.
